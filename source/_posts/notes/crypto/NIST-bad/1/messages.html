16:56
I see that NIST has made a somewhat arbitrary distinction between "finalist" and "alternate" third-round candidates, essentially based on whether NIST thinks you could drop them into TLS in all contexts where TLS is used with no design changes.
16:58
I'm honestly not interested in NIST's opinion on that, and I don't think other cryptographic engineers will be either. All of the "finalist" and "alternate" algorithms should just have been third-round candidates
17:03
There are more applications than are dreamt of in NIST's philosophy, and some of those applications have no problem with moderately large (tens of kilobytes) signatures or ciphertexts.
17:13
It's not even clear that TLS, ssh, etc. have a problem with that. We could get rid of bloated certificate chains and their flaky, exploitable parsers, rip out the whole CA infrastructure that is deeply subverted anyway, and replace it with something that actually can give users confidence in security. That would save more than enough bandwidth to allow for somewhat larger signatures, ciphertexts, and/or public keys.
17:15
After all, if you're worried about quantum computers then WHY AREN'T YOU worried about CAs being owned (and pwned) by bad actors?
