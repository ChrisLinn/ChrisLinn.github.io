Hi, thanks for inviting us and for introducing our paper GKSS20 to the channel @Omershlo ! Since also the paper of Gennaro and Goldfeder (GG20) was recently discussed in this group, let us offer a few bits of comparison from our perspective.

Although both papers propose protocols for tECDSA with accountable faults, they differ considerably when it comes to utilized techniques and achieved properties. In short: GG20 has better accountability during the setup and fewer rounds in the signing phase, and our protocol scales better and has a more robust signing phase, i.e., not only it identifies faults, but is able to produce correct signatures in spite of faults. For a more detailed comparison we refer to Section 2.6 of our paper, and Section 1.1 of GG20 (yet that one is slightly outdated, as it refers to the old version of our paper which did not include benchmarks).

Of course, we encourage everyone to take a look at our paper https://eprint.iacr.org/2020/498 and are happy to answer any questions!

To see these two thresholds in action, consider the case of decentralized custody with t=2n/3. There are basically two things that can go wrong in such systems:
1. Adversary can abort the whole system, and effectively burn all funds that are in the custody (i.e., griefing attack). To do so, adversary would need to control 1/3N nodes here.
2. Adversary can steal all of the funds. To do so, in the example, adversary would need to control 2/3N nodes.
So it seems reasonable to choose high values of t, although significantly smaller than N, to make the cost of griefing attacks also substantial.

Adam Gagol
I'll just quickly come back to the heated discussion above, hopefully to summarize it. I‚Äôve read the GG20 paper again and I agree with @sgoldfed that the protocol indeed can be easily transformed into a version with effective setup and attributability during the signing phase, my bad, sorry. I think that the updated comparison should be as follows:

1. GG20 is able to attribute faults during the setup without revealing private key, at the cost of using a broadcast channel for every message. We can‚Äôt do that, we would have to reveal priv key for such attributability to happen (what in some deployment scenarios is not that bad, above and in the paper we show how to make very reasonable decentralized custody with it, but sometimes it‚Äôs ofc very bad).

2. GG20 has one round signing phase at the cost of slightly weaker, yet still very reasonable, ECDSA assumption (where randomizing element is revealed before the message to be signed is known), our protocol has three round signing phase with standard ECDSA assumption.

3. Our protocol has a robust signing phase for any threshold t, meaning that for a chosen t it is enough that t honest nodes are active to sign a message. On the other hand, t adversarial nodes can sign malicious messages, so it is in a way two-thresholds setup (t and N-t). GG20 has attributability during the signing phase, meaning that it is enough that one node is uncooperative for the signing to fail, but such a node is then revealed by means of an additional protocol. After such failure, a new setup and signing without revealed node need to be performed.

Summing up, I believe that none of these papers provides a strictly stronger result, they make very different design choices, hence arrive at different properties. For example, we didn't consider using a broadcast channel for MtA, as at the first glance it seemed prohibitively inefficient for a high number of nodes. Whether our robust ‚Äúdouble-threshold‚Äù setup is better or worse than fully attributable but easy to abort GG20 is also a matter of debate, although here I would stay with our choice. ;)
