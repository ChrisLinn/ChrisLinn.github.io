The initial round is required for the security proof. This is how a simulator is able to make sure that the output public key of the DKG is the same as it was given. The simulator will wait for the other parties to commit, and then rewind and change its commitment. At the next round the simulator will simulate its VSS without knowing the ec dlog of the free term. The Schnorr proof is required against rogue key attack. `

Alternatively the first round could have been a round of commitments to the non-hiding commitment of the free term : comm(a_0G), which at the second round be used in the VSS (V0 = a_0G). This is the DKG gg18 are using.
In both cases, as @kobigurk suggested, there is a possibility of bias but it doesn’t impose an issue on the security proof.
Additionally, common to both, is the dishonest majority assumption, we referred to it here as “happy flow” meaning we assume no party cheats, but if a party cheats, there is no way to fix it, only to detect and start the protocol from the beginning .
